Research

We study the geophysical fluid dynamics (GFD) of the ocean and atmosphere, using theory, state-of-the-art high-resolution numerical models, and observations. A particular focus of our work is the ocean submesoscale (horizontal scales of 0.1-10 kms), a range of scales which are particularly challenging for traditional observational and computational methods. Understanding the dynamics of the ocean submesoscale is currently one of the most outstanding challenges in ocean physics, with fundamental connections to large-scale ocean circulation, ocean primary productivity, and air-sea interaction.

Bottom boundary layer

Submesoscale processes in the ocean bottom boundary layer

Submesoscale instabilities, fronts, and eddies are a prominent part of the dynamics of the surface boundary layer, with a wide-range of well documented effects on ocean dynamics, biology, and biogeochemistry. However, despite key similarities between surface mixed layers at a front and bottom boundary layers (BBLs) over topography, relatively little is known about the effect of submesoscale turbulence on BBL dynamics.

Our recent work on this topic has shown that the full range of submesoscale instabilities can develop in the BBL. This finding has implications for how nutrients and pollutants are mixed in coastal regions, the energetics of the ocean circulation, and how the deep abyssal waters make their way back to the ocean surface. Many of these topics are the subject of active ongoing research, with available PhD and postdoctoral projects (please contact me for more information).

Multi-scale dynamics

From turbulence to the submesoscale to the gyre scale

The submesoscale occupies a unique part of the spectrum of ocean motions, contributing to both the inverse and forward cascade of energy. While we have made significant progress on our understanding of the dynamics at this scale, an outstanding challenge in physical oceanography is understanding the multi-scale aspects of submesoscale flows. How do submesoscales interact with, and modify, Langmuir circulations and turbulence? What are the cumulative effects of the submesoscale on larger-scale ocean circulation? These questions are particularly important in light of the fact that we will not directly resolve the submesoscale (or smaller) in climate models for at least the next several decades. In the interim, understanding the multi-scale dynamical pathways through which the effects of the submesoscale act is key to improving our understanding and modeling of the ocean circulation.

Some of the specific topics we are currently working on include how ocean fronts modify the dispersion of tracers (such as nutrients or pollutants), how frontal instabilities modify turbulence near the surface, and how the interaction of submesoscales-Langmuir-turbulence affect entrainment and subduction. Right now is a particularly exciting time to work on these problems as increased computational power, and the development of new observational instruments, are continually opening new lines of inquiry. Please get in touch if you would like to discuss opportunities to get involved.

Air-sea interaction

Coupled interaction between the atmosphere and ocean

Upper ocean processes exert strong controls on the atmospheric boundary layer, with changes in sea-surface temperature modifying surface winds and heat fluxes, coupling the ocean and atmosphere. Much of our understanding of these coupled dynamics was derived considering the ocean mesoscale (horizontal scales of approximately 10 - 100 kms), and the atmospheric response to smaller-scale ocean variability is not well understood. Recently we have used very high resolution Large-Eddy Simulations to examine how submesoscale fronts can strongly modify the structure of winds and temperature in the atmospheric boundary layer, in ways that are not currently captured in most ocean and atmosphere models.

These topics are the subject of ongoing work, with the potential to help refine our understanding of air-sea interaction and coupling across scales, as well as to improve forecasting of the marine atmospheric boundary layer, with possible applications to future satellite observing missions.

Jacob Wenegrat

Assistant Professor
Department of Atmospheric and Oceanic Science
University of Maryland, College Park

I am a physical oceanographer, with a research focus on the ocean submesoscale (horizontal scales of 0.1-10 km). These small spatial scales are particularly challenging for traditional observational and computational methods, and understanding processes at these scales remains among the most pressing open challenges in oceanography and geophysical fluid dynamics. In my work I use a combination of theory and state-of-the-art high-resolution numerical simulations, with the goal being to gain deep insight into physical processes in the ocean and atmosphere. At the University of Maryland I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Atmospheric and Oceanic Science, and am affiliate faculty in Applied Mathematics & Statistics, and Scientific Computing, as well as the Burgers Program for Fluid Dynamics. I am also an editor for the Journal of Physical Oceanography, and co-chair of the US CLIVAR Process Study and Model Improvement Panel (PSMIP).

I did my undergraduate work at Stanford University, in an interdiscplinary program called Symbolic Systems, where I was primarily interested in what science could tell us about classic questions in mind-body philosophy. After college I worked for several years in technology consulting, and then spent 2 years living and traveling by sailboat. During this time I became interested in the field of oceanography, and became particularly excited by how theory, observations, and technology were being used together to answer fundamental questions about our earth system. I then did my PhD in Oceanography (with Mike McPhaden), and an MS in Applied Mathematics, at the University of Washington. For my postdoctoral work I returned to Stanford, where I worked with Leif Thomas on submesoscale ocean dynamics.
